Cultural Wealth Reference Card

You have been building wealth your whole life.

Reference + Reflection  |  Based on Dr. Tara J. Yosso's Community Cultural Wealth Framework  |  Section 1: Your Leadership Story

Dr. Tara Yosso's Community Cultural Wealth framework challenges the idea that students of color lack resources. Instead, it names six powerful forms of capital that communities of color carry and develop — often without recognizing them as leadership strengths. Click any card to expand it and reflect on how that form of wealth shows up in your own life.

Framework by: Dr. Tara J. Yosso, University of California — Race Ethnicity and Education, 2005
The Six Forms of Cultural Wealth
Capital 01
🌟
Aspirational Capital
The ability to hold onto hope and dreams for the future — even in the face of real barriers.
Your capacity to dream beyond your current circumstances and believe a better future is possible — for yourself and your community.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Being the first in your family to pursue higher education
  • Setting goals others told you were unrealistic
  • Keeping going when the system made things hard
  • Encouraging others to dream bigger
Leadership connection
Aspirational capital fuels Inspiring a Shared Vision — the ability to imagine a better future and bring others along with you toward it.
✦ My Reflection
Where has aspirational capital shown up in your life? What have you continued to hope for despite obstacles?
Capital 02
🗣️
Linguistic Capital
The intellectual and social skills developed through communication in multiple languages and styles.
Your ability to communicate across languages, dialects, and contexts — including code-switching, storytelling, and translating for others.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Speaking more than one language or dialect
  • Translating for family members in official settings
  • Knowing how to read a room and adjust your communication
  • Using storytelling, humor, or metaphor to connect with people
Leadership connection
Code-switching is not a weakness — it is a sophisticated leadership skill. Linguistic capital supports enabling others to act by helping you communicate across difference and build bridges.
✦ My Reflection
How has your ability to communicate across languages or styles been a strength? When has it helped you lead or connect?
Capital 03
🏡
Familial Capital
The cultural knowledge, values, and wisdom nurtured through family and community ties.
The deep sense of responsibility, care, and belonging cultivated through your family — biological, chosen, or community-based.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Learning values like loyalty, sacrifice, and care from family
  • Taking on responsibility for siblings, elders, or community members
  • Carrying forward family traditions, stories, and wisdom
  • Feeling accountable to people beyond just yourself
Leadership connection
Familial capital builds deep empathy and accountability. Leaders with this strength know how to care for a team, protect community, and make decisions that honor those who came before.
✦ My Reflection
What values or lessons has your family or community given you? How do those show up in how you lead or care for others?
Capital 04
🤝
Social Capital
The networks of people, peers, and community members that provide instrumental support.
The relationships, connections, and community you have built — and your ability to use those networks to support yourself and others.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Being a connector — knowing who to call and who needs what
  • Building trust across different communities and groups
  • Showing up for your people consistently
  • Mobilizing a group around a shared need or goal
Leadership connection
Social capital is the foundation of coalition-building. Leaders with this strength know how to bring people together, build trust, and create movements from relationships.
✦ My Reflection
Who are your people? How have your relationships and networks supported you — or helped you support others?
Capital 05
🧭
Navigational Capital
The skills developed through maneuvering through social institutions not designed with you in mind.
Your ability to figure out systems, institutions, and environments that were not built for people like you — and help others do the same.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Figuring out college applications, financial aid, or workplace systems on your own
  • Helping family members navigate bureaucracy or institutions
  • Learning unwritten rules that no one explained to you
  • Being the guide for others coming up behind you
Leadership connection
Navigational capital makes you a powerful advocate and mentor. You know what it takes to survive and thrive in systems — and you can use that knowledge to clear paths for others.
✦ My Reflection
What systems or institutions have you had to figure out on your own? What did that teach you about resilience and resourcefulness?
Capital 06
✊
Resistant Capital
The knowledge and skills developed through opposition to inequality and the maintenance of dignity.
Your understanding — drawn from lived experience — of what is unjust, and your commitment to challenging it and standing up for what is right.
Explore this capital ▼
What it looks like
  • Speaking up when something isn't right — even when it's hard
  • Refusing to accept less than you or your community deserves
  • Organizing, advocating, or protecting others
  • Maintaining your dignity and values under pressure
Leadership connection
Resistant capital fuels challenging the process — the courage to question the status quo, take stands, and lead change even when it's uncomfortable or risky.
✦ My Reflection
When have you challenged something unfair or stood up for yourself or others? What did that take — and what did it teach you?
