The Same Way Every Year

Your campus organization plans events the same way every year. Attendance is low, especially from students of color. The president says, "We've always done it this way." You have ideas about what might reach more people — but you don't want to overstep as a newer member. You don't have a formal title or an official leadership role.

Apply → CHALLENGE THE PROCESS
Before you respond: Think about what you observed in your Critical Consciousness activity and what you practiced in the Problem → Possibility Reframe. This scenario is your chance to apply both. Work through each question in order — they build on each other.
1
→ What data or stories would support your idea most powerfully?
Critical consciousness means backing your instincts with evidence. What have you observed? What stories from students might you gather? Is there any research on what makes events accessible and inclusive? Whose lived experience makes this case?
0 characters
2
→ How do you raise the issue in a way that protects your relationship with the president?
The goal isn't just to be right — it's to stay in the room and move the idea forward. What does the president care about? How can you frame your concern as aligned with their goals, not in opposition to them? What tone and timing would open doors rather than close them?
0 characters
3
→ What's the smallest experiment you could propose to test your idea?
Change agents don't ask for everything at once. What's one low-risk, low-cost test that could show whether your idea works — without requiring anyone to abandon the whole existing approach? Think: one event, one small change, a 30-day pilot, a single conversation.
0 characters
4
→ How do you frame this as "adding to" the organization's mission — not criticizing it?
From the lecture: frame your challenge as a contribution, not a complaint. How does reaching more students support what this organization already says it stands for? What language signals that you're on the same team — even while pushing for change?
